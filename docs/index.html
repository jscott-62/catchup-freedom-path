What inflation does to your number

Inflation is the slow rise in prices over time. A dollar today does not buy what it bought 20 years ago. The same is true going forward: by the time you retire in 15 years, the dollars in your retirement account will buy less than they do now.

Why we show two return numbers

Your investment statement will report one return (for example, 21%). That is the number on paper.

But inflation eats into that growth. If your account grew 21% but prices went up 3%, only 18% of that growth actually bought you more in real life.

  • 21%: what your investments need to grow on paper each year
  • 18% after inflation: what your real buying power needs to grow

What inflation rate should you pick?

  • The government's official measure (CPI) usually says 2-3%
  • Many people believe real cost-of-living inflation, especially in housing, healthcare, and food, runs more like 6-10%
  • For a stress test, slide up to 10% and see what happens
